## Why Business Owners Shouldn’t Build Their Own Websites (Mostly)

Can you build your own website?  
Technically, yes.

Should you?  
*Probably not.*

Here’s why:

### 1. **You’re too close to your own business**
It’s hard to communicate your value clearly when you’re emotionally attached to every detail.  
Professionals know how to simplify, sharpen, and guide customers where they need to go.

### 2. **DIY websites often look DIY**
Even with drag-and-drop tools, it’s easy to accidentally create:

- weird spacing  
- uneven layouts  
- clashing fonts  
- fifty shades of not-quite-your-brand-colour  

Customers *can* tell.

### 3. **You’ll waste hours on things a designer solves in minutes**
Learning curves. Trial and error.  
YouTube rabbit holes that steal entire weekends.

Your time is worth more than that.

### 4. **SEO, accessibility, structure, UX — all get ignored**
Most DIY sites struggle with:

- slow loading  
- missing alt text  
- broken mobile layouts  
- messy menus  
- weak keywords  

A professional bakes all of this in automatically.

### 5. **Your website is your first impression**
If it looks unprofessional, people assume the business is the same.

So yes — use these platforms.  
But don’t confuse “no code needed” for “no expertise needed.”

## The Best Website Builders (and Who They’re Actually For)

Let’s skip the fluff and get straight into it.

---

## Squarespace — The Stylish All-Rounder

Squarespace is the platform for people who want something clean, modern, and beautiful — quickly.

### Why it’s great
- Stunning templates that look good *out of the box*  
- Easy drag-and-drop editing  
- Perfect for small service businesses  
- Built-in SEO, analytics, email marketing tools  
- Affordable monthly costs  

### When it’s ideal  
Portfolios, salons, coaches, creatives, local businesses.

### The catch  
Custom design still requires expertise.  
DIY layouts often end up looking *almost* right… but not quite.

---

## Wix — Flexible, Friendly, but Easy to Overdo

Wix is the “you can literally drag anything anywhere” platform.  
This is both its superpower *and* the reason many Wix sites look chaotic.

### Why it’s great
- True drag-and-drop freedom  
- Hundreds of templates  
- Built-in apps for bookings, events, members  
- Easy to get started  
- Good for budget-conscious businesses  

### When it’s ideal  
Startups, hobby sites, small businesses with simple needs.

### The catch  
Because there’s no rigid structure, inexperienced users often create cluttered, inconsistent pages.  
Good design requires… well, design.

---

## Shopify — Best for Selling (Even If You Don’t Code)

If your business involves e-commerce, Shopify is the clear winner.

### Why it’s great
- Highest converting checkout  
- Amazing templates  
- Professional product management  
- Secure, fast hosting  
- Apps for anything you can imagine (subscriptions, bookings, bundles)  

### When it’s ideal  
Anyone selling physical or digital products.

### The catch  
Shopify’s builder is simple but not magic — to make it look polished, cohesive, and on-brand, you’ll want a designer.

---

## Webflow — The “Designer’s Playground”

This is the platform everyone on Twitter raves about.  
It’s powerful, flexible, and wildly customisable — without code (mostly).

### Why it’s great
- Pixel-perfect design control  
- CMS capabilities for blogs, portfolios, dynamic pages  
- Smooth animations and interactions  
- Clean, semantic code output  

### When it’s ideal  
Established businesses, tech companies, design-driven brands.

### The catch  
Webflow is *not* beginner-friendly.  
A DIY Webflow site is basically a rite of passage into chaos.

## So Which One Should You Use?

Simple:

- **Squarespace** → You want something beautiful, cohesive, and easy to update.  
- **Wix** → You want extreme flexibility and a lower budget.  
- **Shopify** → You want to sell products professionally.  
- **Webflow** → You want a high-end, custom website and you’re not afraid to hire help.  

But whichever platform you choose — remember this:

**Your website builder matters, but your website designer matters more.**

Because even the best platform can’t fix:

- unclear messaging  
- messy layouts  
- poorly sized images  
- badly structured pages  
- confusing navigation  

That’s the work of someone who does this all day, every day.
